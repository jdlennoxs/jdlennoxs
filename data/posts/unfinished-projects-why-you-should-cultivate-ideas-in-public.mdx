You might be reading this, some time after I wrote it, on a beautiful, finished website, packed with the functionality and content that the ambitious side of my brain wants to implement. Or, you may be looking at the same website I am currently looking at, an imperfect and clearly unfinished slice of the internet. Why not wait until the latter has become the former? What would cause me to release an unfinished website?

## Start small

The adage of 'start small' is excellent advice, however the discipline to stick to that advice is something I have struggled with time and again.

I have been creating a personal website in some form or another since 2018 and as you can probably tell, it hasn't ever really gone to plan. As a developer and budding technologist, I enjoy tinkering, exploring and creating. An unfortunate side effect of this is a tendency to continually search for something new.

I'm sure many other developers can corroborate on the stigma of perfectionism, not wanting to release something until all the kinks have been ironed out. Picking a technology for its simplicity and quick time to viable product just before getting caught up in the fact that it doesn't do x or there is a newer and shinier y now available.

The purpose of this website has always been to act as a place to host my personal projects and maybe share some of my thoughts and experiences from my career as a developer and manager. Importantly, neither of those things require a bespoke web application, it requires content.

## Building in public

I have been using Obsidian as a second brain to capture knowledge from content I consume and my own ideas for a little over a year. Given that I am already generating a fair amount of content here, this seems like the perfect source of content for my personal website and presents the opportunity for my own 'digital garden'.

A digital garden, as popularised by developers such as Maggie Appleton, is an alternative way to organise and present written content. Unlike blogging, the idea is to grow ideas; from small seeds of insight to evergreen sources of knowledge. One of the important tenets of digital gardening is the idea that this cultivation shouldn't take place behind closed doors but in a live, living document, available to anyone.
