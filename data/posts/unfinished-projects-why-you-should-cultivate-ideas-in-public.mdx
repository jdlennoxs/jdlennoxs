You might be reading this, some time after I wrote it, on a beautifully finished website, packed with the functionality and content that the ambitious side of my brain wants to implement. Or, you may be looking at the same website I am currently looking at, an imperfect and clearly unfinished slice of the internet. Why not wait until the latter has become the former?

Who would release an unfinished website?

## Start small

I have been creating a personal website in some form or another since 2018 and as you can probably tell, it hasn't ever really gone to plan. As a developer and budding technologist, I enjoy tinkering, exploring and creating. An unfortunate side effect of this is a tendency to continually search for something new. The adage of 'start small' is excellent advice, however the discipline to stick to that advice is something I have struggled with time and again.

I'm sure many other developers can corroborate on the stigma of perfectionism, not wanting to release something until all the kinks have been ironed out. Picking a technology for its simplicity and quick time to viable product just before getting caught up in the fact that it doesn't do x or there is a newer and shinier y now available.

The purpose of this website has always been to act as a place to host my personal projects and maybe share some of my thoughts and experiences from my career as a developer and manager. Importantly, neither of those things require a bespoke web application, it requires content.

## Building in public

A digital garden, as popularised by developers such as Maggie Appleton, is an alternative way to organise and present written content. Unlike blogging, the idea is to grow ideas; from small seeds of insight to evergreen sources of knowledge. One of the important tenets of digital gardening is the idea that this cultivation shouldn't take place behind closed doors but in a live, living document, available to anyone.

But this doesn't apply purely to blogging. The same can apply to budding artists, musicians or web developers. The value of sharing your work as you go for me has three main factors.

### Accountability

You don't have to go it alone.

You may have an intrinsic motivation to do something you love, but the chances of following through to a finalised idea is often a far more treacherous journey than just getting started.

Work does not take place in isolation, it usually comes in the form of collaborative decision-making and delegation and by agreeing to achieve an expected outcome, you are made accountable.

So how can we achieve the same thing in our personal endeavours?

You may have heard of an _accountability partner_, someone to hold you to account of your results, and building in public seems like an effective partner for this.

A study by The American Society of Training and Development (of which I could not find an actual copy) suggests that by committing to someone that you will to do _it_, you are 65% likely to achieve _it_.

We can see evidence of this approach in other places, such as Duolingo, who - while also benefitting from gaining users - want you to add friends and share your targets with them.

Building in public tells the world, "I am going to try and do this thing".

### Motivation

Look at where you are compared to where you started from.

Setting yourself an ambitious task often results - in my experience - in an unfinished project. As time goes on with nothing to show for your labours, it's easy to lose the drive to carry on.

Cultivating your idea in public creates a trail of real deliverables, turning a large task into smaller ones and identifying the things that will help you grow faster.

Be proud of what you've achieved and use that to fuel further development.

### Inspiration

Great ideas don't often happen in a vacuum.

When you get your ideas, your art or your music under the scrutiny of the public eye, you get something wonderful in return. Feedback.

Feedback may not always be what you want to hear - see motivation - but this can also be spark that unblocks your creative path or sets you in a new direction.

A similar approach is used in business when developing new products for market. By going public early, you can correct or change your end goal frequently, without running into sunk cost fallacy.

## Build with me

Hopefully this clears up why this website may or may not be barely finished. By creating this post I state my intent, to develop and grow my thoughts in writing, and also draw a marker in the sand for the first truly public iteration of my website.

Content is king, and preparing for this post alone has already opened my eyes to the fact I was chasing the wrong goals even if I had the right intentions.

My main takeaway for anyone reading this post is to show someone that thing you've been working on.

You don't have to host a website to share something with the world. You don't even have to share it with the world.

Instagram, Twitter, YouTube or Medium all provide free ways to make the same commitment.

You can even just show it to your mum.

I just wish I'd taken the plunge sooner.
